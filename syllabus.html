Course Description

This course examines Japanese popular culture as a dynamic arena where the nation narrates itself—its past, present, and imagined futures. Through manga, anime, film, music, theater, and other media, we will explore how cultural texts curate collective "self talk," shaping identity and social imagination. The first module of the semester investigate popular retellings of pivotal historical episodes. Retellings in popular forms such as the ones we study often are more impactful than what we learn in history textbooks in forming a narrative of our collective past. In module two, we look at the way memory, dreams, and the supernatural have a special (and culturally specific) relationship with time. Module three turns to the future and explores the way that science fiction narratives reflect on the context of their own creation. Together, these case studies highlight a central paradox: stories of the past and future are always and only about the present. Along the way, we will probe the ephemeral nature of pop culture, the shifting border between high and low culture, and the question of what constitutes "pop culture" itself. Students will gain tools to critically analyze cultural production while reflecting on how Japan's narratives resonate globally and locally in contemporary life.

This course satisfies requirements for the Asian Studies Certificate and the Japanese Major and Minor.

Learning Outcomes

Knowledge of Humanities and Arts GER Outcomes

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

  1. Analyze diverse perspectives and experiences by examining how Japanese popular culture reflects identities shaped by class, gender, region, and global audiences.
    • Assessment: Scaffolded “They Say / I Say” assignments require students to summarize, respond, and extend scholarly arguments about cultural texts, demonstrating their ability to engage multiple perspectives.
  2. Examine the historical development of identity and citizenship by investigating how popular retellings of historical episodes narrate evolving concepts of belonging and civic participation in Japan.
    • Assessment: Scaffolded assignments guide students from identifying cultural narratives to constructing arguments about their implications.
  3. Investigate differential experiences and outcomes by considering how marginalized groups—such as women, youth subcultures, or regional communities—are represented in and excluded from cultural narratives.
    • Assessment: Students use “They Say / I Say” templates to articulate counter arguments and highlight underrepresented voices in pop culture.
  4. Analyze social movements and cultural change by exploring how cultural texts engage with activism and reform efforts (e.g., labor, anti nuclear, gender equality) and their impact on civic discourse.
    • Assessment: Scaffolded assignments culminate in a persuasive essay where students connect cultural texts to civic movements.
  5. Compare cultural systems and perspectives by contrasting Japanese cultural narratives of citizenship and civic identity with those of other nations.
    • Assessment: Final scaffolded assignment requires students to situate Japanese pop culture within comparative frameworks, demonstrating synthesis of course outcomes.

Assignment Testing GER Outcomes

Three scaffolded writing assignments will be incorporated into the examinations. These assignments are based on They Say / I Say: The Moves That Matter in Academic Writing, with Readings, by Cathy Birkenstein, Gerald Graff, and Russel Durst:

  • Project 1 (Summary & Response): Students practice identifying arguments in cultural texts and identifying appropriate responses.
  • Project 2 (Counter Argument & Evidence): Students identify arguments and articulate their own responses.
  • Project 3 (Synthesis Essay): Students construct a sustained argument connecting Japanese pop culture to civic identity, social movements, and comparative perspectives.

Together, these scaffolded assignments directly assess GER learning outcomes while embedding HIPs such as writing intensive practice, collaborative learning, diversity/global learning, and undergraduate research.

By the end of this course students should be able to:

  1. Identify the most prominent figures, genres, and media that pertain to the production of Japanese popular culture. Students will be able to explain how these shaped subsequent artistic, literary, and performative traditions.
  2. Explain how popular cultural genres such as manga, anime, film, music, and theater became canonical within popular and performative traditions, and examine how patterns of production and consumption of these works have changed over time.
  3. Utilize critical frameworks and methods of close reading to analyze cultural values transmitted through Japanese popular culture, including questions of identity, citizenship, and civic participation.
  4. Present their analysis through low stakes quizzes, in class activities, projects, and written exams. Students will be able to construct logical arguments based on evidence provided by Japanese media and cultural narratives.

UW System Shared Learning Goals

The above learning outcomes support the UW System learning goals. These goals, and the assignments which most directly support them, are as follows:

  1. Knowledge of Human Cultures and the Natural World: Students will engage with a broad survey of Japanese cultural history through manga, anime, film, music, and theater. They will be assessed through weekly quizzes and written exams on their ability to place these texts in historical and civic contexts.
  2. Critical and Creative Thinking Skills: Students will be trained in close reading and interpretive analysis of cultural artifacts. These skills will be assessed through scaffolded assignments, including group projects and in class activities.
  3. Effective Communication Skills: Students will use critical frameworks to make arguments about cultural texts and artifacts, and present those arguments in discussion, writing, and collaborative formats.
  4. Intercultural Knowledge and Competence: Students will engage with cultural material that is novel to most of them and from a background they do not share. They will be asked to empathize with and understand diverse perspectives, and to connect Japanese narratives to broader civic and global contexts.

Credit Hour Policy Time Allocation

This is a three-credit course. As a general principle, students should expect to spend at least two hours of preparation work for every one hour spent in class. Thus, students should expect to spend three hours in class and six hours in preparation each week, for a total of nine hours per week.

Module 2: Dream Time, Memory, and Supernatural Time

Objectives: In this unit, we look at the weird ways that time plays out in dreams, the spirit world, and memory. We’ll also probe the popular culture/high culture divide from the perspective of This unit will fast forward to the Edo Period, with a particular focus on the fall of the Tokugawa Shogunate and the Meiji Restoration. This is a particularly exciting time in history (and it is a time when a young Tom Cruise visited the last samurai!), and it is a past that is particularly present in contemporary Japan. We’ll engage with the Shinsengumi, a band of sanctioned thugs who ‘defended’ Kyoto for the shogunate. This group is perhaps the single most popular episode in contemporary Japan for many decades. We’ll talk about why that might be. We’ll think about the difference between fiction and historiography and talk about why that matters.

Course Policies

Late Assignments

This is an asynchronous course, but it is designed with as much student interaction as possible. This requires, among other things, that students engage regularly and within the deadlines. We may not be co-present for this class, but we can engage with each other and share insights by working with and sticking to shared deadlines. Because of this, it is incumbent upon each student to complete units and modules by the assigned deadline. It is the responsibility of the student to have a functioning with stable internet access. Technical questions can be directed to the UWM Tech desk at 414-229-4040.

Safety Policies

For students with health or safety concerns, please contact the instructor. We are dedicated to making the classroom a safe and accessible place for all students.

Make-up Policies

Students are responsible for obtaining the material from missed classes. As stated above, missed in-class quizzes and assignments cannot be made up. However, fifteen weeks is a long time, and it is totally understandable that things can come up. Because of this, the two lowest quiz scored are dropped, no questions asked. You don’t need to provide doctor’s notes or other excuses. Exams, however, can only be taken at a later date in the case of a documented and unavoidable emergency.

Digital Tools Policy

There are tools that are easily available that might seem like they offer easy shortcuts to the work in this class. There are so many of them and new ones emerge so quickly that any examples I share today will be old news by next week. I encourage you not to make use of them. Anything that may seem like a short-cut is by definition not a replacement for the work we do in this class. This class has been carefully designed to teach you very specific and tangible skills, and having taught this class for many years (and revised and improved it in every new iteration), I can promise you that you have a wonderful opportunity this semester to gain analytical skills that you can take with you for the rest of your life. Using computer-generated summaries, online videos, digital writing assistants, or other shortcuts will not only deprive you of building these skills, they will also inevitably result in very disappointing results in this particular class. I encourage you to set aside the time in your week to do the preparation for every unit. Shortcuts may seem efficient in the short term, but you will find that productivity is far more rewarding than efficiency any day of the week.

tl;dr: using ChatGPT, YouTube, Wikipedia, etc won’t help you in this class!
